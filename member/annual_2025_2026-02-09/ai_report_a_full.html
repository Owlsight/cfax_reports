Executive Summary

Haiti's import trade in 2025 demonstrated measured growth amid shifting supplier dynamics and evolving commodity patterns. Based on CargoFax bill of lading (BOL) data, the following key findings emerged:

  • Total tonnage reached 4.09 billion kg, up 4.3% from 3.92 billion kg in 2024, reflecting steady demand growth despite ongoing economic challenges.

  • Container throughput expanded significantly, with TEU volume climbing 29.5% to 101,500 TEU from 78,300 TEU the prior year—a notably faster pace than tonnage growth, suggesting a shift toward containerized goods.

  • China emerged as the year's standout performer, with tonnage surging 83.0% to 312.4 million kg while TEU volume jumped 67.1% to 27,100 TEU, capturing 26.7% of Haiti's containerized import market (up from 21.6% in 2024).

  • The United States remained Haiti's largest supplier but lost ground, with tonnage declining 6.5% to 1.17 billion kg even as TEU volume rose 33.9% to 27,500 TEU—a divergence driven by shifts in bulk commodity flows.

  • Port-au-Prince handled 68.1% of all tonnage and 72.5% of TEU, reinforcing its dominance as Haiti's primary gateway, while Cap-Haïtien saw TEU volume grow 36.0% to 18,200 TEU.

  • Evergreen emerged as the fastest-growing major carrier, with tonnage up 80.5% and TEU volume rising 87.4% to 18,300 TEU, capturing 18.0% market share in containerized trade.

    • Bottom line: Haiti's 2025 import profile reveals a market in transition—containerization is accelerating, China is gaining share across multiple metrics, and energy product flows are reshaping traditional supplier relationships. The modest 4.3% tonnage growth masks significant structural shifts beneath the surface.

    Part I: The Big Picture

    Haiti's import trade in 2025 grew at a measured pace, with total tonnage reaching 4.09 billion kg, up 4.3% from 3.92 billion kg in 2024. This modest expansion belies more significant shifts in how goods arrived and from where they originated.

    The most striking development was the acceleration in containerized trade. TEU volume climbed 29.5% to 101,500 TEU from 78,300 TEU the prior year—a growth rate nearly seven times faster than tonnage expansion. This divergence suggests Haiti's import mix is gradually shifting toward higher-value, containerized goods and away from bulk commodities that move in larger volumes per shipment.

    📊 Dual-axis chart showing tonnage (bars
    and TEU (line) by month, 2024 vs 2025)

    Shipment counts reinforced this containerization trend. Total shipments reached 43,400, up 32.5% from the prior year, while container movements totaled 58,800, rising 29.5% year-over-year. The growth in shipment frequency outpacing tonnage growth indicates more frequent, smaller consignments—a pattern consistent with diversifying supply chains and potentially more fragmented ordering patterns.

    Vrac (bulk/break-bulk) shipments presented a more nuanced picture. Total vrac movements reached 12,500, up 11.6% from 2024. While this growth rate lagged containerized metrics, it nonetheless represented continued demand for commodities that move outside standard container formats—primarily petroleum products, cement, and grains.

    The quarterly pattern revealed meaningful seasonality. The first quarter saw tonnage decline 2.4% year-over-year to 860.5 million kg, reflecting a slow start to the year. The second and third quarters then accelerated, with tonnage rising 14.2% and 12.5% respectively. The fourth quarter softened again, declining 4.6% to 1.15 billion kg. TEU volumes, by contrast, grew in every quarter, with the third quarter posting the strongest expansion at 58.1% year-over-year.

    This macro picture—modest tonnage growth, accelerating containerization, and quarterly volatility—sets the stage for understanding the commodity and country dynamics that drove Haiti's 2025 import profile.

    Part II: Top Commodities

    Haiti's import basket in 2025 remained concentrated in essential commodities: energy products, construction materials, and food staples. However, the relative weights shifted meaningfully, with several product categories posting notable gains or declines.

    Petroleum Products: The Dominant Category

    Petroleum oils (HS 2710) represented the single largest commodity flow by tonnage, reaching 670.2 million kg—approximately 16.4% of Haiti's total imports. The U.S. Virgin Islands supplied the majority at 519.9 million kg, up 48.8% from 2024, while the United States itself emerged as a significant new source, shipping 136.9 million kg compared to negligible volumes the prior year.

    This shift in petroleum sourcing represents one of the year's most significant trade developments. The U.S. Virgin Islands' St. Croix refinery continued its role as Haiti's primary fuel supplier, but the emergence of direct U.S. mainland shipments—primarily from Houston and New Orleans—suggests either capacity constraints in the Virgin Islands or competitive pricing from Gulf Coast refineries.

    Crude petroleum (HS 2709) flows also shifted dramatically. The Netherlands Antilles emerged as the dominant supplier at 28.9 million kg, up 363.9% from 6.2 million kg in 2024, while U.S. Virgin Islands crude shipments collapsed 88.6% to just 6.0 million kg. This substitution pattern warrants monitoring, as it may reflect changing refinery relationships or pricing dynamics in the Caribbean basin.

    📊 Stacked bar chart showing petroleum product flows by origin country, 2024 vs 2025

    Cement: Diversifying Supply Base

    Portland cement (HS 2523) remained Haiti's second-largest import category by tonnage, reaching approximately 1.05 billion kg. The supplier landscape, however, underwent significant restructuring.

    Turkey retained the top position at 449.9 million kg, though this represented a modest 2.5% decline from 2024. Egypt held steady at 236.5 million kg, essentially flat year-over-year. The notable shifts came from emerging suppliers:

  • Vietnam surged 161.6% to 149.1 million kg, reaching an all-time high
  • Dominican Republic jumped 686.5% to 117.2 million kg
  • Panama expanded 541.9% to 64.4 million kg, also an all-time high
  • United States emerged from near-zero to 36.1 million kg

    • Meanwhile, Colombia collapsed from 29.3 million kg to just 24,200 kg—a 99.9% decline that effectively removed it from Haiti's cement supply chain.

    This diversification appears driven by both demand growth and supply chain resilience considerations. The emergence of regional suppliers (Dominican Republic, Panama) alongside continued Asian flows (Vietnam) suggests importers are actively managing supplier concentration risk.

    Grains: Rice and Wheat Dynamics

    Rice (HS 1006) imports reached 477.3 million kg, with the United States dominating at 345.0 million kg (72.3% share), down modestly 2.6% from 2024. Pakistan, the second-largest supplier, saw volumes decline 26.6% to 104.7 million kg, while India grew 47.2% to 21.1 million kg.

    The Pakistan decline and India gain suggest some sourcing substitution within the Asian rice market, though the United States' dominant position remained stable. In containerized terms, however, India led with 940 TEU (49.8% share), followed by Pakistan at 643 TEU—reflecting the different shipping modes used by each origin.

    Wheat (HS 1001) presented a different story. Imports surged 135.2% to 220.7 million kg, all sourced from the United States. This represented an all-time high for U.S. wheat shipments to Haiti, suggesting either increased domestic milling capacity or substitution away from imported flour toward domestic processing.

    Poultry: Strong Growth from Multiple Origins

    Poultry meat (HS 0207) imports reached 87.2 million kg, with the United States leading at 55.9 million kg—up 110.4% from 2024 and representing an all-time high. Brazil contributed 20.1 million kg, also an all-time high at 67.2% growth. Colombia added 3.3 million kg of new volume.

    In TEU terms, poultry represented 6,600 TEU, with the United States at 4,200 TEU (63.8% share) and Brazil at 1,500 TEU (23.0% share). The strong growth across multiple origins suggests robust demand expansion rather than simple sourcing substitution.

    Sugar: Regional Concentration

    Sugar (HS 1701) imports totaled approximately 175.4 million kg, sourced primarily from Central American and South American origins. Guatemala led at 49.2 million kg (28.1% share), up 24.2%, followed by Colombia at 47.9 million kg (27.3% share), down 18.9%. El Salvador, Honduras, and Brazil rounded out the top five.

    Nicaragua's sugar exports to Haiti collapsed 84.2% to just 6.9 million kg from 43.6 million kg—one of the year's most significant single-origin declines. This drop was partially offset by Guatemala's gains, but the overall sugar supply chain appears to be consolidating around fewer, larger suppliers.

    Mixed Cargo and Unspecified Goods

    The catch-all category (HS 9999) for commodities not specified according to kind reached 283.8 million kg in tonnage and 22,200 TEU in containerized volume. The United States dominated at 207.4 million kg (73.1% share) and 13,700 TEU (61.5% share), though U.S. tonnage declined 27.7% while TEU volume rose 46.9%.

    China's share of this category grew notably, with tonnage up 103.7% to 55.7 million kg and TEU up 90.5% to 5,600 TEU. This category often captures consolidated shipments of consumer goods, household items, and other mixed merchandise—suggesting China's growing role in supplying Haiti's consumer economy.

    Part III: Country Dynamics

    The country-level picture in 2025 reveals a market in transition, with China gaining share across nearly every metric while traditional suppliers like the United States and Turkey saw mixed results.

    United States: Still Dominant, But Losing Ground in Tonnage

    The United States remained Haiti's largest import partner by a significant margin, but its position showed signs of erosion. Tonnage declined 6.5% to 1.17 billion kg, representing 28.6% market share—down from 29.9% in 2024. This 1.3 percentage point share loss, while modest, continues a multi-year trend of gradual U.S. share erosion.

    The tonnage decline, however, masked strength in containerized trade. U.S. TEU volume rose 33.9% to 27,500 TEU, capturing 27.1% market share. Shipments grew 32.5% to 22,300, representing 51.3% of all shipments—meaning more than half of all individual consignments arriving in Haiti originated from the United States. Container movements rose 39.1% to 14,700, and vrac shipments grew 11.6% to 11,700.

    The divergence between declining tonnage and rising containerized metrics reflects the commodity mix shift discussed earlier. The United States lost significant volume in bulk categories (particularly the collapse in public transport vehicles from 147.5 million kg to 8.6 million kg) while gaining in containerized consumer goods and food products.

    U.S. goods arrived through multiple origin ports, with Port Everglades, FL leading in shipments (9,700) and TEU (12,800), while New Orleans, LA led in tonnage (385.1 million kg) driven by grain shipments. Houston, TX contributed 302.4 million kg and 2,900 TEU, while Miami, FL handled 10,100 shipments—the highest shipment count of any origin port.

    China: The Year's Standout Performer

    China's expansion across all metrics represented the most significant country-level development of 2025. Tonnage surged 83.0% to 312.4 million kg, lifting market share from 5.0% to 7.6%—a gain of 2.7 percentage points.

    The containerized metrics were even more striking. TEU volume jumped 67.1% to 27,100 TEU, capturing 26.7% market share (up from 21.6%). This means China now rivals the United States in containerized import volume, despite handling less than one-third of U.S. tonnage. Shipments rose 66.5% to 8,400, and containers grew 70.9% to 14,800.

    What drove China's surge? The commodity drill-down reveals several factors:

  • Mixed cargo (HS 9999) rose 103.7% to 55.7 million kg
  • Steel bars and rods (HS 7214) exploded from 169,700 kg to 19.8 million kg—an 11,560.7% increase
  • Motorcycles (HS 8711) grew 197.8% to 12.8 million kg
  • Iron/steel articles (HS 7326) rose 205.3% to 11.2 million kg
  • Wire and cables (HS 7312) jumped 870.0% to 6.5 million kg

    • This product mix—construction materials, vehicles, and consumer goods—suggests China is capturing share in Haiti's infrastructure and consumer markets simultaneously. The steel bar surge is particularly notable, as it indicates either new construction demand or substitution away from other steel suppliers.

    U.S. Virgin Islands: Energy Dominance Continues

    The U.S. Virgin Islands maintained its role as Haiti's primary petroleum supplier, with tonnage rising 30.4% to 526.4 million kg—representing 12.9% of Haiti's total imports. Virtually all of this volume (519.9 million kg) consisted of petroleum oils from the St. Croix refinery.

    The territory's containerized presence remained minimal (20 TEU, 185 shipments), reflecting its specialized role as an energy supplier rather than a diversified trade partner. All shipments originated from St. Croix, which served as the single origin port for this trade relationship.

    Turkey: Steady Cement Supplier

    Turkey held its position as Haiti's third-largest supplier by tonnage at 471.7 million kg, essentially flat year-over-year at -0.8%. Market share declined modestly from 13.5% to 11.5% as other suppliers grew faster.

    Turkey's trade with Haiti remained concentrated in cement (449.9 million kg), with Iskenderun serving as the primary origin port (294.2 million kg). TEU volume rose 29.2% to 652, and containers grew 38.0% to 476, suggesting some diversification beyond bulk cement into containerized goods.

    Dominican Republic: Regional Partner Gains Share

    The Dominican Republic emerged as a notable gainer, with tonnage rising 39.8% to 281.3 million kg and market share climbing from 5.7% to 6.9%. TEU volume grew 37.9% to 4,300, shipments rose 49.7% to 1,600, and containers increased 41.2% to 2,200.

    The commodity breakdown reveals the drivers:

  • Cement (HS 2523) surged 686.5% to 117.2 million kg
  • Steel structures (HS 7308) jumped from near-zero to 15.7 million kg
  • Prepared binders (HS 3824) grew 35.2% to 36.3 million kg

    • Meanwhile, petroleum oils (HS 2710) collapsed 83.8% to 10.7 million kg, suggesting the Dominican Republic is transitioning from an energy intermediary to a construction materials supplier for Haiti.

    Rio Haina served as the primary origin port, handling 193.8 million kg—up 205.8% from 2024. The proximity advantage (shared island of Hispaniola) appears to be driving increased trade in heavy construction materials where shipping costs matter significantly.

    Vietnam: Cement Surge Drives Growth

    Vietnam posted the strongest percentage growth among major suppliers, with tonnage surging 131.4% to 160.0 million kg. Market share rose from 2.0% to 3.9%, a gain of 1.9 percentage points.

    Cement dominated the commodity mix at 149.1 million kg, up 161.6% and representing an all-time high. Halong served as the primary origin port at 149.2 million kg, up 161.7%. The containerized metrics showed more modest growth (TEU up 12.4% to 911), reflecting cement's bulk shipping characteristics.

    Egypt: Stable Cement Supplier

    Egypt maintained steady volumes at 246.9 million kg, up 3.4%, with cement comprising the vast majority at 236.5 million kg. TEU volume jumped 358.0% to 442, suggesting some diversification into containerized goods, while Alexandria served as the primary origin port at 131.7 million kg.

    Indonesia and Pakistan: Diverging Paths

    Indonesia held relatively steady at 112.2 million kg (+5.4%), with TEU essentially flat at 2,100. The country supplies a diversified basket including ceramics, textiles, and food products.

    Pakistan, by contrast, declined 26.1% to 105.6 million kg, driven primarily by the rice decline discussed earlier. TEU fell 16.5% to 728, and containers dropped 20.6% to 647. Market share fell from 4.0% to 2.6%, a loss of 1.5 percentage points.

    Brazil: Steady Diversified Supplier

    Brazil grew modestly at 96.3 million kg (+9.4%), with TEU up 5.3% to 6,000. The country supplies a diversified mix including poultry (20.1 million kg, all-time high), sugar (19.9 million kg), ceramics (12.6 million kg), and processed meats (9.8 million kg).

    Part IV: Carrier & Shipping Analysis

    The carrier landscape in 2025 featured significant market share shifts, with Evergreen emerging as the fastest-growing major line while private carriers continued to dominate bulk movements.

    Private Carriers: Bulk Movement Specialists

    Private carriers collectively handled the largest share of Haiti's import tonnage, reflecting the importance of bulk commodities (petroleum, cement, grains) that move outside standard container shipping.

    PRIVATE CARRIER - RVAM led all carriers with 850.5 million kg (+4.7%), representing 20.8% of total tonnage. This carrier handled 452 vrac shipments, suggesting specialization in bulk petroleum or cement movements.

    NYK LINE ranked second at 520.3 million kg (+8.5%), capturing 12.7% market share with 68 vrac shipments. PRIVATE CARRIER - QSA followed at 350.5 million kg (-6.3%), while PRIVATE CARRIER - MOS handled 315.9 million kg (+9.1%).

    These private carriers operate zero TEU and zero containers, confirming their exclusive focus on bulk/break-bulk cargo.

    Container Lines: Evergreen's Surge

    Among containerized carriers, Evergreen posted the strongest growth, with tonnage surging 80.5% to 210.3 million kg and TEU jumping 87.4% to 18,300 TEU. This performance lifted Evergreen's TEU market share to 18.0%, making it the largest container line serving Haiti by this metric.

    Shipments rose 85.8% to 4,900, and containers grew 81.7% to 10,400. The consistency of growth across all metrics suggests Evergreen gained share through both new customer acquisition and increased volumes from existing relationships.

    Mediterranean Shipping Co (MSC) also posted strong growth, with tonnage up 46.4% to 244.5 million kg and TEU rising 45.1% to 17,200 TEU (17.0% market share). Shipments surged 62.9% to 4,100, and containers grew 51.0% to 10,900.

    CMA/CGM presented a mixed picture. Tonnage declined 10.2% to 227.8 million kg, while TEU held essentially flat at 17,500 TEU (17.2% market share). Shipments grew 28.5% to 5,100, but containers fell 4.2% to 10,500. This divergence suggests CMA/CGM may be handling more LCL (less-than-container-load) shipments or experiencing cargo mix shifts.

    ZIM Lines grew across most metrics, with tonnage up 87.6% to 71.0 million kg and TEU rising 75.9% to 6,800 TEU (6.7% market share). Containers jumped 80.9% to 3,700.

    Accordia Shipping showed dramatic shipment growth at 1,804.5% to 1,700 shipments, though tonnage grew only 1.6% to 113.0 million kg. This pattern suggests the carrier is handling many small consignments, possibly in the consolidation or freight forwarding segment.

    National Shipping of America: Multi-Metric Growth

    National Shipping of America emerged as a notable multi-metric gainer, with tonnage up 161.5% to 10.3 million kg, TEU rising 188.3% to 836, shipments growing 384.6% to 63, and containers jumping 241.7% to 656. While still a small player by absolute volume, this growth rate suggests the carrier is actively expanding its Haiti service.

    Part V: Port Analysis

    Haiti's port infrastructure remained concentrated around Port-au-Prince, though secondary ports showed meaningful growth in 2025.

    Ports of Arrival: Port-au-Prince Dominates

    Port-au-Prince handled 2.79 billion kg of imports, up 7.5% from 2024, representing 68.1% of Haiti's total tonnage. TEU volume reached 73,500 (+32.8%), capturing 72.5% market share. The port processed 26,000 shipments (59.8% share) and 42,700 containers (72.7% share).

    The port's vrac volume, however, declined 40.8% to 5,000 shipments, suggesting some bulk cargo may be diverting to other facilities or that the commodity mix is shifting toward containerized goods.

    Cap-Haïtien, Haiti's second port, handled 727.0 million kg (+1.2%), representing 17.8% of tonnage. TEU volume grew 36.0% to 18,200 (18.0% share), while shipments surged 52.4% to 8,400. Vrac shipments jumped 146.6% to 1,200, suggesting the port is capturing more bulk traffic.

    Lafito emerged as a growing containerized gateway, with tonnage up 48.8% to 304.1 million kg (7.4% share). TEU held steady at 9,700 (9.6% share), while shipments grew 35.1% to 2,800. The port's vrac volume surged 260.0% to 36 shipments, though from a small base.

    Saint Louis du Sud handled 165.7 million kg of bulk cargo with zero TEU, indicating specialization in break-bulk commodities—likely cement or other construction materials.

    Miragoane presented a notable pattern: tonnage collapsed 71.6% to 104.0 million kg, yet shipments surged 133.1% to 4,400 and vrac grew 133.0% to 4,400. This suggests the port shifted from fewer large bulk shipments to more frequent smaller consignments.

    Gonaïves showed explosive growth from a tiny base, with tonnage up 5,695.6% to 1.3 million kg and shipments rising 32,200.0% to 323. Les Cayes similarly emerged with shipments up 40,200.0% to 403. These patterns suggest new trade routes or port facilities coming online.

    Ports of Origin: Geographic Diversity

    Haiti's imports originated from a geographically diverse set of ports, reflecting the country's varied supplier base.

    St. Croix (U.S. Virgin Islands) led in tonnage at 526.4 million kg (+30.4%), handling petroleum shipments almost exclusively.

    New Orleans, LA ranked second at 385.1 million kg (+31.8%), driven by grain exports (wheat, rice). The port handled 512 TEU and 191 shipments, with 56 vrac movements.

    Houston, TX contributed 302.4 million kg (+5.0%) with a more diversified cargo mix, including 2,900 TEU and 598 shipments.

    Iskenderun (Turkey) handled 294.2 million kg (+36.9%) of primarily cement cargo.

    Port Everglades, FL showed a notable divergence: tonnage declined 49.9% to 221.8 million kg, yet TEU surged 42.2% to 12,800 and shipments jumped 95.5% to 9,700. This pattern suggests the port is handling more containerized consumer goods and fewer bulk commodities.

    Rio Haina (Dominican Republic) grew 205.8% to 193.8 million kg, reflecting the Dominican Republic's emergence as a construction materials supplier.

    Halong (Vietnam) reached 149.2 million kg (+161.7%), driven entirely by cement exports.

    Miami, FL handled the highest shipment count at 10,100 (+1.9%) and 7,100 vrac movements, though tonnage was modest at 86.6 million kg. This pattern reflects Miami's role as a consolidation hub for smaller consignments.

    Part VII: Top Outliers & Anomalies

    The 2025 data contains several significant outliers that warrant attention, both for their magnitude and their potential implications.

    Largest Absolute Movers

    U.S. Virgin Islands → Petroleum Oils (HS 2710): Volume rose from 349.5 million kg to 519.9 million kg (+48.8%), reaching an all-time high. This 170.4 million kg increase represents the largest absolute gain in the dataset.

    USA → Public Transport Vehicles (HS 8702): Volume collapsed from 147.5 million kg to 8.6 million kg (-94.2%). This 138.9 million kg decline represents the largest absolute loss, likely reflecting the completion of a specific vehicle import program or fleet renewal cycle.

    USA → Petroleum Oils (HS 2710): Volume surged from 19,900 kg to 136.9 million kg (+687,395.4%), reaching an all-time high. While the percentage change is extreme due to the small base, the absolute volume (136.9 million kg) is significant and represents a new trade flow.

    USA → Wheat (HS 1001): Volume rose from 93.9 million kg to 220.7 million kg (+135.2%), reaching an all-time high. This 126.8 million kg increase suggests either increased domestic milling capacity or substitution from imported flour.

    Dominican Republic → Cement (HS 2523): Volume surged from 14.9 million kg to 117.2 million kg (+686.5%). This 102.3 million kg increase reflects the Dominican Republic's emergence as a regional cement supplier.

    Conclusion

    Haiti's 2025 import profile reveals a market undergoing meaningful structural transition. The headline 4.3% tonnage growth masks several significant developments:

    First, containerization is accelerating. The 29.5% TEU growth rate—nearly seven times faster than tonnage expansion—indicates Haiti's import mix is shifting toward containerized goods. This trend has implications for port infrastructure, shipping line strategies, and the types of products entering the Haitian market.

    Second, China is gaining share across nearly every metric. The 83.0% tonnage surge and 67.1% TEU growth lifted China's market share meaningfully, with the country now rivaling the United States in containerized import volume. The commodity mix—steel, motorcycles, consumer goods—suggests China is capturing share in both infrastructure and consumer markets.

    Third, supplier diversification is occurring in key categories. The cement market's shift away from Turkey-Egypt dominance toward Vietnam, the Dominican Republic, and Panama reflects active supply chain management by Haitian importers. Similar patterns appear in rice (India gaining from Pakistan) and petroleum (U.S. mainland emerging alongside U.S. Virgin Islands).

    Fourth, regional suppliers are gaining importance. The Dominican Republic's 39.8% tonnage growth and Panama's 315.9% surge (driven by cement) suggest proximity advantages are becoming more valuable—whether for cost, reliability, or risk management reasons.

    Fifth, the United States remains dominant but faces erosion. While still Haiti's largest supplier by a significant margin, the United States lost 1.3 percentage points of tonnage share and saw absolute tonnage decline 6.5%. The shift toward containerized goods (TEU up 33.9%) may partially offset this trend, but the direction is clear.

    For supply chain professionals, these patterns suggest several considerations: monitoring China's continued expansion, evaluating regional sourcing options for construction materials, and tracking the containerization trend's implications for logistics and distribution.

    Methodology Note

    This report is based on bill of lading (BOL) data compiled by CargoFax, covering all recorded import shipments arriving at Haitian ports during calendar year 2025. The data includes:

  • Tonnage (kg): Weight of goods as declared on bills of lading
  • TEU: Twenty-foot equivalent units, measuring containerized cargo volume
  • Shipments: Individual consignment records
  • Containers: Physical container movements
  • Vrac: Bulk and break-bulk shipments outside standard containers

    • Limitations:

  • BOL data captures declared weights and may differ from actual weights
  • Some shipments may be recorded under catch-all categories (HS 9999)
  • Private carrier designations may aggregate multiple operators
  • Year-over-year comparisons reflect the data available in each period

    • Data processing:

  • Country and commodity totals exclude any aggregated "total" rows to prevent double-counting
  • Percentage changes are calculated from prior-year values; extreme percentages (>1,000%) typically reflect small bases
  • Market share calculations use the current period's total as the denominator
